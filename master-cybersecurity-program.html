Section 1 — Executive Overview

1.1 Purpose of This Program

The purpose of the Master Cybersecurity Program is to establish a comprehensive, enforceable and operationally safe security architecture for Syntra Refining Corporation. This document defines the principles, controls, processes and responsibilities required to protect Syntra Refining industrial facility, its information systems, operational environments and critical intellectual property.

The program applies to all personnel, all contractors and third parties, all internal and external systems, all physical and digital infrastructure and all cyber-physical equipment including refining equipment, instrumentation, sensors and safety systems. The program does not rely on external connectivity for core operations and is specifically engineered for fully offline, critical-infrastructure environments.

1.2 The Offline Facility Security Principle

The Syntra Refining industrial facility is designed and operated as offline sites. No internet connectivity exists inside any production, laboratory or refining network. No outbound or inbound traffic traverses the public internet. No Virtual Private Network tunnels, cloud management channels or Software-as-a-Service dependencies exist within facility systems.

All essential operations—including refining, analytics, safety systems, quality control, Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition and Programmable Logic Controller control processes—function independently from any external network. This offline model eliminates remote ransomware, external exploitation, cloud mis-configuration incidents that rely on internet-delivered payloads.

1.3 Zero-Trust Security Approach

Even within an offline environment Zero-Trust Architecture is applied. Zero-Trust assumes that:

  • Every user must be continuously authenticated
  • Every device must be verified and compliant
  • Every action must be authorised based on role and context
  • Every system must enforce least-privilege and segmentation
  • Every data movement must be controlled and monitored

Zero-Trust is implemented across identity, device, network, application, data, Operational Technology, Internet of Things and physical systems. The combination of Zero-Trust Architecture and offline facility design forms a hardened, safety-driven security environment suitable for refining operations.

1.4 Alignment with Industrial Safety

Human safety is prioritised above all other objectives.

Cyber-security controls must never compromise industrial safety. Safety interlocks, Safety Programmable Logic Controllers and emergency-shutdown logic operate independently. Operational processes remain stable during security events. No cyber-security measure may disable essential equipment or interfere with regulatory safety requirements.

1.5 Security Objectives

Core Security Objectives

  • Rejection of External Attack Vectors: Isolation from the internet and cloud systems removes remote attack surfaces
  • Protection of Critical Data: All data—operational, scientific, financial and strategic—is retained within controlled environments
  • Preservation of Industrial Continuity: No outage of external services can disrupt Syntra Refining industrial facility
  • Assurance of Controlled Change: No software, firmware or configuration changes may occur without documented, approved and supervised processes
  • Incident Prevention and Containment: Threats are detected, contained and resolved while maintaining safe plant operation

Section 2 — Foundational Security Principles

2.1 Principle 1 — Offline Industrial Environment

The Syntra Refining industrial facility operates without any public network access. There is no public Domain Name System resolution, no public Internet Protocol routing, no cloud Application Programming Interfaces, no external email services, no external messaging systems and no service requiring real-time cloud connection.

The facility runs entirely on local servers, local authentication systems, local network routing, local storage and local Safety and Distributed Control Systems. This environment cannot be bridged to the internet without physical re-configuration and executive authorization.

2.2 Principle 2 — Segmented Architecture

The facility is divided into independent networks: Information Technology, Operational Technology, Internet of Things, Industrial Demilitarized Zone and Physical Security Network. Segmentation is enforced with hardware firewalls, Access Control Lists, physical cable separation, restricted routing tables and one-way data-transfer mechanisms.

2.3 Principle 3 — Identity Assurance

Authentication depends on hardware-based Multifactor Authentication tokens, local directory services, Tier-0 identity controls for senior and privileged accounts and device posture validation that is enforced offline through Group Policy Objects or Mobile Device Management policies applied prior to facility deployment.

2.4 Principle 4 — Device Trust and Hardening

Every authorised device must be fully encrypted, enable Secure Boot, contain approved configurations only, display no unauthorised ports or services and be registered in the offline asset inventory.

2.5 Principle 5 — Operational Safety Priority

The cyber-security program shall never override safety relay function, Safety Programmable Logic Controller logic, Emergency-Stop behaviour or hazardous-equipment shutdown rules.

Cyber-security follows the requirement: "Do not introduce new hazards."

2.6 Principle 6 — Controlled Information Movement

Because the facility is fully offline, all data movement must occur through encrypted offline media, one-way data diodes, controlled export points with dual authorization and documented transfer logs. No information may exit or enter without explicit approval.

2.7 Principle 7 — Continuous Monitoring and Auditing

Even offline systems require strong monitoring through local logging servers, local Security Information and Event Management ingestion nodes, physical security logs, Operational Technology historian logs and offline integrity-analysis tools. Aggregated logs may be exported periodically for external analysis.

Section 3 — Enterprise Security Architecture

3.1 Overview

The Enterprise Security Architecture describes how Syntra Refining industrial facility enforce operational safety, data integrity and cyber-resilience while remaining completely isolated from the public internet. It incorporates full offline operation, Zero-Trust controls, segmentation of every technology domain, physical and logical isolation of critical workloads, local-only authentication, compute and storage and controlled data export using encrypted offline mechanisms.

3.2 Enterprise Architectural Domains

3.2.1 Information Technology Domain

Local intranet services, authentication and directory services, administrative workstations, data-storage servers, internal document management, facility-management applications, offline collaboration services and local printing and shared resources. The Information Technology environment operates strictly offline with no access to the public internet or cloud services.

3.2.2 Operational Technology Domain

All industrial control systems: Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition systems, Programmable Logic Controllers, Safety Programmable Logic Controllers, Human-Machine Interface terminals, distributed sensors and actuators, industrial network switches and historian data servers. Must physically and logically isolated from all other systems and must maintain continuous operation independently of the Information Technology domain or external systems.

3.2.3 Internet of Things Domain

Environmental sensors, temperature and humidity sensors, vibration and equipment-maintenance sensors, access-control sensors, on-site camera systems and local monitoring gateways. All Internet of Things systems operate locally and do not communicate with cloud platforms; they report only to internal Intranet of Things gateways within the industrial facility.

3.2.4 Physical Security Domain

Camera systems and Network Video Recorder servers, door-access systems, badge readers, environmental controls and alarms, fire-suppression monitoring systems and visitor-management systems. Physical security is tightly integrated with cyber controls to ensure complete situational awareness across the facility.

3.2.5 Industrial Demilitarized Zone Domain

Separates Information Technology and Operational Technology networks. Contains jump servers, patch-staging servers, log-aggregation servers, offline update-distribution nodes and controlled one-way data-transfer systems. The Industrial Demilitarized Zone does not route to the internet and does not accept inbound or outbound external traffic.

3.2.6 Executive and Administrative Domain

Executive computing devices, administrative review systems, document vaults and high-sensitivity data storage. These systems operate using Tier-0 identity protections and must comply with the highest internal security standards.

3.3 Separation of Network Environments

Strict Segmentation Model

  • No routable pathways between domains unless explicitly defined
  • Firewalls enforce deny-by-default rules
  • Access Control Lists enforce least-privilege routing
  • Physical cable isolation for sensitive networks
  • Wireless connectivity restricted to permitted local-only channels

Segmentation ensures that compromise in one environment cannot spread into another.

3.10 Network Isolation Model

Complete Isolation Strategy: All networks—Information Technology, Operational Technology, Internet of Things, physical security and management infrastructure—are prevented from communicating with external networks including the public internet and external cloud platforms.

Isolation Achieved Through

  • Physically disconnected external interfaces
  • Non-routable internal addressing
  • Hardened firewall enforcement
  • Removal of all outbound and inbound routes
  • Strict Access Control Lists
  • Local-only Domain Name System services

The offline network model ensures that compromise of any external system cannot propagate into any Syntra Refining industrial facility.

Section 4 — Operational Security Management

4.1 Overview

Operational Security Management defines how Syntra Refining Corporation governs cyber-security and ensures disciplined, repeatable execution of all security processes across the Syntra Refining industrial facility. The purpose is to establish clear authority, defined roles and responsibilities, standardised security procedures, continuous operational readiness and coordination between Information Technology, Operational Technology and physical-security teams.

4.2 Governance Structure

Executive leadership is responsible for approving cyber-security policies, allocating resources, reviewing audit reports, assessing risk exposures and ensuring regulatory compliance. The Chief Executive Officer and the Technology Director maintain ultimate accountability.

4.6 Change Management Framework

Because the Syntra Refining industrial facility operates offline and contain safety-critical systems, mismanaged changes can cause disruptions, safety failures or data corruption.

Every Change Must Include

  • Change-request submission
  • Risk assessment including Operational Technology safety impact
  • Technical review with Information Technology and Operational Technology engineering involvement
  • Testing in air-gapped environment
  • Approval from Information Technology Security and Operational Technology Engineering
  • Safety-engineering approval where required
  • Execution during authorised maintenance windows
  • Post-change validation and documentation
  • Sign-off

Section 5 — Monitoring and Incident Response

5.1 Overview

Monitoring and Incident Response ensure that Syntra Refining Corporation can detect security events, contain incidents quickly, preserve evidence, protect industrial processes and maintain safe and continuous operation. Because the Syntra Refining industrial facility operates fully offline all monitoring solutions must function without external connectivity, retain logs locally, operate autonomously and support offline analysis.

5.5 Incident Categories

Level 1 – Minor Incident

Failed login attempts, misconfigured workstation, non-critical alarm.

Action: Local review and documentation.

Level 2 – Moderate Incident

Suspicious Operational Technology device behaviour, unauthorised removable-media use, unexpected Programmable Logic Controller network scan.

Action: Investigation by local Security Operations Center, escalation to Technology Director if needed.

Level 3 – High Severity Incident

Unauthorised Programmable Logic Controller logic modification, Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition configuration tampering, suspicious intermittent disconnection of sensors, detection of malware on Information Technology servers.

Action: Containment, joint Information Technology/Operational Technology response, notification to executive leadership.

Level 4 – Critical Industrial Incident

Compromise of Safety Programmable Logic Controller logic, ongoing manipulation of Operational Technology control traffic, attempted sabotage of refining operations, physical intrusion targeting control equipment.

Action: Immediate activation of emergency-response protocols including facility lockdown if required, Operational Technology Engineering takeover, safety-interlock verification, executive-team engagement and preservation of evidence.

Section 6 — OT & Industrial Control Systems Security

6.1 Purpose and Scope

This binder defines all security, safety and operational requirements for Operational Technology and Industrial Control Systems in all Syntra Refining industrial facility. It applies to Programmable Logic Controllers, Safety Programmable Logic Controllers, Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition systems, Human-Machine Interface systems, industrial instrumentation, sensors and actuators, industrial networking equipment, historian systems, engineering workstations, alarm-management systems and batch-processing systems.

6.2 Operational Technology Architectural Principles

  • Safety First: No cyber-security control may interfere with emergency shutdown, hardwired interlocks, Safety Programmable Logic Controller logic execution, operator-alarm visibility or Safety Instrumented Systems
  • Deterministic Behaviour: Operational Technology systems must produce predictable outputs, must not exhibit timing beyond acceptable tolerance, must not depend on remote resources and must not alter execution without authorised intervention
  • Offline Autonomy: Operational Technology systems must continue operating indefinitely without internet, retain full functionality during Information Technology outages and maintain all supervisory and safety behaviour offline
  • Segmentation: Operational Technology networks must be physically isolated, logically isolated, protected by firewalls and free of unnecessary pathways

Section 7 — Executive Cybersecurity Program

7.1 Purpose

The Executive Cybersecurity Program ensures that high-level personnel at Syntra Refining Corporation—including executive leadership, board members, senior engineers and senior administrators—are protected against credential theft, device compromise, targeted attacks, insider threats, social engineering, sensitive data exposure and physical risks.

7.2 Tier-0 Identity Classification

Tier-0 identities are accounts with the highest administrative access and control directory services, Certificate Authority, offline Key Management System, Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition administrative functions, Operational Technology configuration privileges and Executive Vault access.

Section 8 — AI Integration in Offline Environments

8.1 Artificial Intelligence Philosophy for Syntra Refining

Syntra Refining Corporation uses Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning to support human operators, engineers and analysts, enhance detection of anomalies, faults and risks and optimise maintenance and operations.

Artificial Intelligence never replaces Safety Programmable Logic Controllers, hardwired interlocks, emergency-shutdown systems or human safety decisions. Artificial Intelligence is advisory only, not a control authority.

8.12 Summary of AI Integration Rules

  • Advisory, not control: Artificial Intelligence never sends direct commands to Programmable Logic Controllers or Safety Programmable Logic Controllers
  • No internet: All Artificial Intelligence compute on-premises, offline
  • Train outside, run inside: Data goes out, models come back in via controlled offline channels
  • Strong governance: Artificial Intelligence changes treated like Operational Technology changes, with multi-team approval
  • Explainability preferred: Where possible models must explain why they triggered an alert
  • Safety first: If Artificial Intelligence conflicts with safety logic or human safety, safety wins

Section 9 — Appendices

9.1 Glossary of Technical Terms

Operational Technology: Technology used to monitor or control physical industrial processes including Programmable Logic Controllers, Safety Programmable Logic Controllers, Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition and Human-Machine Interface systems.

Information Technology: Digital systems supporting business administration, document management and computing systems inside a facility.

Industrial Control System: Collective term for Operational Technology equipment managing industrial processes including Programmable Logic Controllers, Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition, Human-Machine Interfaces, sensors and control networks.

Zero-Trust Architecture: Security model that assumes no user, device or network is trusted by default, requiring continuous verification.
